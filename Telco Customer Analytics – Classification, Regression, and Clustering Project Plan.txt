Telco Customer Analytics – Classification, Regression, and Clustering Project Plan
Dataset Overview and Source
We will use the Telco Customer Churn dataset (available on Kaggle) for this project[1]. This real-world styled dataset comes from a fictional telecommunications company and contains data on 7,043 customers in California over one quarter[2]. Each row represents a unique customer and there are 21 features describing each customer’s demographics, services, and account information[3]. Key attributes include:
	•	Customer status: whether the customer churned (left the service) or not (target for classification)[4]. In this dataset, 1,869 customers out of 7,043 (~26.5%) churned, indicating an imbalanced class distribution[5].
	•	Services signed up: e.g. Phone service, multiple lines, Internet service type (DSL, fiber, etc.), and add-on services like online security, device protection, tech support, streaming TV/movies[6].
	•	Account details: tenure (months with the company), contract type (month-to-month, one-year, two-year), payment method (e.g. electronic check, credit card), paperless billing, monthly charges, total charges to date[7][8].
	•	Demographics: gender, senior citizen status, partner, and dependents[9].
This rich dataset provides a business context in the telecom domain, allowing us to tackle multiple ML tasks. We can perform predictive modeling and extract insights relevant to customer retention and revenue – a common real-world business scenario.
Project Objectives and Problem Statements
Our goal is to demonstrate a full machine learning pipeline on this dataset, encompassing three types of tasks: classification, regression, and clustering. Below are the defined problem statements for each task:
	•	Classification – Customer Churn Prediction: Build a model to predict whether a customer will churn (Yes/No) based on their profile (services, account tenure, demographics, etc.). This will help the company identify at-risk customers and devise retention strategies. Churn prediction is a critical business problem – for example, customers on month-to-month contracts have much higher churn rates (~42.7%) than those on two-year contracts (~2.8%)[10], so a predictive model can target such high-risk groups.
	•	Regression – Customer Lifetime Value (CLTV) / Revenue Prediction: Use regression to predict a continuous value of interest. One approach is to predict the total charges a customer will accumulate (as a proxy for customer lifetime value) based on their initial features. This helps in understanding revenue drivers and identifying high-value customers. Alternatively, we could predict a customer’s monthly charges from their subscribed services (effectively modeling the pricing structure) or a satisfaction score if it were available. For this project, we will treat TotalCharges (total revenue from a customer to date) as the target for regression, enabling us to estimate customer value and identify factors that increase revenue.
	•	Clustering – Customer Segmentation: Perform unsupervised clustering to group customers into distinct segments based on their attributes and behavior. The goal is to discover customer segments (e.g. “contract-loyal bundle users” vs “low-tenure single-service users”) that can inform marketing and service strategies. For instance, clustering can reveal groups of customers with similar service usage or demographics, which the company can target with specialized offers. This customer segmentation task will use features like tenure, services, and charges to find natural groupings in the customer base.
These three tasks cover a comprehensive analytics workflow: churn classification addresses a binary outcome, revenue regression addresses a continuous outcome, and clustering uncovers patterns without a predefined label. Together, they provide a 360° analysis of the business problem.
Data Preprocessing and Feature Engineering
Before modeling, we will perform thorough data preprocessing to clean and prepare the dataset for analysis. Key steps include:
	•	Data Loading: Download the dataset (CSV file) from Kaggle directly into Google Colab. For example, use Kaggle’s API or an open URL to retrieve Telco-Customer-Churn.csv. Load it into a pandas DataFrame and inspect the first few rows to understand the structure.
	•	Handling Missing and Invalid Values: Check for missing or invalid entries in each column. In this dataset, the TotalCharges column is read as a string and has some blank values for customers with zero tenure (new customers). We will convert TotalCharges to numeric and set invalid blanks to NaN, then impute or drop as appropriate[11]. Since the number of blanks is small (they occur only for recently joined customers), we can impute them as 0 (assuming no charges yet for 0 months tenure) or drop those records to simplify.
	•	Type Conversions: Ensure each feature has the correct data type. For example, convert the SeniorCitizen flag from integer to a categorical (Yes/No) for consistency, and confirm that MonthlyCharges and TotalCharges are numeric.
	•	Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA): Perform an initial exploration to understand distributions and relationships:
	•	Plot histograms for numeric features like tenure, monthly charges, total charges to see their distributions (e.g., tenure might show a spike at 0-1 months due to new customers).
	•	Plot bar charts for categorical features (contract type, internet service, payment method, etc.) to see the frequency of each category.
	•	Visualize the relationship between each feature and churn. For example, we can plot churn rate by contract type (as seen, month-to-month customers have much higher churn) and churn rate by payment method (electronic check users had ~45% churn vs ~15% for automatic payments[12]). Such visual EDA helps identify which factors correlate with churn.
	•	Compute a correlation matrix for numeric features (tenure, charges, etc.) and visualize it (heatmap) to detect multicollinearity or strong relationships. For instance, we expect TotalCharges to correlate with tenure and MonthlyCharges (longer tenure and higher monthly fees yield higher total charges).
	•	Feature Engineering: Based on insights, create any new features if useful. Possible engineered features:
	•	Tenure groups: bucket the tenure (e.g., 0–12 months, 12–24, etc.) since early-tenure customers might behave differently[13].
	•	Number of services used: combine the binary service columns (online security, backup, device protection, etc.) into a single count of services for each customer (as the data suggests more services -> lower churn[14]).
	•	Average charges: if needed, derive average monthly charges (TotalCharges/tenure for customers with tenure >0) to see if any anomalies exist. These features could enhance model performance or interpretability.
	•	Encoding Categorical Variables: The dataset has many categorical features (mostly with values "Yes"/"No" or a few categories like internet service type, contract type, payment method). We will convert these into a suitable numeric form:
	•	For binary categories (Yes/No), map to 1/0.
	•	For multi-class categoricals (e.g., InternetService with values DSL/Fiber/No), use one-hot encoding to create dummy variables for each category. Ensure we drop one dummy to avoid redundancy or use an ordinal encoding if applicable (though one-hot is safer for non-ordered categories).
	•	Drop the unique identifier customerID as it has no predictive value. After encoding, we will have a purely numeric feature set ready for modeling. (Note: For clustering, if the high-dimensional one-hot encoded data causes issues, we might consider using only key continuous variables or using an algorithm like k-prototypes that handles mixed data. However, our plan will focus on the simpler approach of encoding all features numerically for k-means clustering.)
	•	Feature Scaling: Scale the features to ensure comparability, especially important for algorithms that use distance (K-means clustering, KNN, etc.) or gradient-based methods. We will apply standardization (z-score scaling) to continuous features like tenure and charges so that they have mean 0 and unit variance. Categorical dummy variables that are 0/1 can be left as is (or scaled similarly to treat 0/1 as numeric values). Scaling is crucial because, for example, TotalCharges (which can be in the thousands) would otherwise dominate distance calculations in clustering. Note: We will fit the scaler on the training set and transform the test set to avoid data leakage in supervised learning.
	•	Splitting Data for Modeling: For the classification and regression tasks, split the dataset into training and testing sets (for example, 70% train, 30% test), using a stratified split on the churn label for classification to maintain the ~26% churn ratio in both sets. The test set will be held out to evaluate model performance. We will also consider using a validation split or cross-validation on the training set for model tuning and selection. (The clustering task does not require a train-test split since it’s unsupervised; we can perform clustering on the full set of customers or perhaps only on current customers if we want to segment the active customer base.)
	•	Address Class Imbalance (for Churn): Since churn (Yes) is the minority class (~26%), we will account for this in modeling. Strategies include using balanced class weights in algorithms (many tree-based and logistic models allow this) or applying resampling techniques on the training data (either oversampling the churn cases, undersampling the non-churn, or using SMOTE to synthetically oversample). The goal is to ensure the classifier doesn’t simply bias toward the majority (non-churn) class. We will also use appropriate evaluation metrics (detailed below) to focus on performance for the positive class.
By the end of preprocessing, we will have a clean, enriched dataset ready for modeling: an encoded feature matrix and target labels for classification/regression, and a scaled feature matrix (without labels) for clustering.
Modeling Approach and Algorithms
We will build and evaluate several models, aligning each to the specific task. The choice of algorithms is guided by the dataset size and the nature of the problem, as well as recommendations from similar projects[15]. All modeling will be implemented in Python (pandas, scikit-learn, etc.) within Google Colab. Key modeling steps:
1. Classification Modeling (Churn Prediction)
	•	Models to use: We plan to start with simple baseline models and progress to more complex ones:
	•	Logistic Regression: A baseline linear model for binary classification. It provides interpretable coefficients to gauge feature impact on churn probability.
	•	Decision Tree: A tree model that can capture nonlinear relationships and feature interactions. It’s easy to interpret through rules (e.g., a tree might learn that “if Contract = Month-to-month and Tenure < 12 months, then churn = Yes”).
	•	Random Forest: An ensemble of trees to improve performance over a single tree. Random Forest usually yields higher accuracy and more stable predictions by averaging many trees. It can also provide an estimate of feature importance.
	•	Gradient Boosted Trees (e.g., XGBoost or LightGBM): A boosting model that often achieves top performance on structured data. We may include this for comparison, given enough time, to see if it improves metrics.
	•	(Optional) K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN): a simpler method that could be tried, though with many features it may be less effective. It’s mentioned for completeness but can be expensive on larger data.
These algorithms (logistic, decision tree, random forest, etc.) are all suitable and have been recommended for this dataset[16]. We will likely compare multiple models to see which gives the best balance of precision and recall for churn prediction.
	•	Training procedure: Use the training set to train each model. For models requiring hyperparameter tuning (e.g., tree depth for decision tree, number of estimators for random forest, regularization for logistic), perform grid search or random search with cross-validation on the training data (possibly using stratified k-fold CV to maintain churn ratio in folds). For example, we might tune the max depth of the tree or the C parameter of logistic regression. We will use cross-validation scores (such as average F1 or ROC-AUC) to select the best hyperparameters, then finalize the model.
	•	Feature importance & selection: During modeling, we’ll examine feature importances (for tree-based models) or coefficients (for logistic) to see which features strongly contribute to churn. If we have a very large number of dummy variables after encoding, we might perform some feature selection (e.g., removing features with very low variance or using a backward elimination based on p-values for logistic, etc.). However, given 21 base features, even with dummies it’s manageable to use all. Still, we will remain cautious of multicollinearity (e.g., TotalCharges is almost a product of tenure and MonthlyCharges). We might decide to drop one of such highly correlated features to simplify the model (for instance, use monthly charges and tenure, but not total charges in the classifier to avoid redundancy).
	•	Handling imbalance in training: If using logistic regression or certain tree models, we can apply class weight = "balanced" to give higher weight to churn cases[17]. For tree ensembles, we might use techniques like subsampling or simply rely on performance metrics that focus on the minority class to guide model selection.
	•	Expected outcome: A trained classification model that can predict churn on new data. We anticipate that contract type, tenure, and payment method will be among important predictors (as exploratory analysis suggests). The model will be evaluated on the test set using metrics described later. We will also ensure to avoid overfitting by comparing training vs validation performance and using techniques like regularization (for logistic) or depth limitation (for trees).
2. Regression Modeling (Revenue/CLTV Prediction)
	•	Target definition: For the regression task, we will predict a customer’s TotalCharges (continuous value). This can be interpreted as the revenue contributed by the customer so far (which correlates with their lifetime value, especially if they have churned, it’s their total past value). By modeling this, we gain insight into what drives higher customer revenue. (Note: Customers with longer tenure and more services naturally have higher total charges, so this regression will largely capture those obvious relationships, but it’s a useful exercise in pipeline completeness and could still reveal interesting secondary factors.)
	•	Models to use: We will explore:
	•	Linear Regression: A basic model to establish a baseline. We expect linear regression to capture the broad trend (e.g., higher monthly charges and longer tenure => higher total charges).
	•	Decision Tree Regressor: To capture nonlinear effects or interactions (e.g., maybe total charges grow non-linearly for very long-tenured customers, or different patterns for different contract types).
	•	Random Forest Regressor: An ensemble approach to improve on single tree performance and reduce overfitting. Random forests can model complex relationships and usually improve predictive accuracy.
	•	Possibly Gradient Boosting Regressor or other advanced regressors if needed for better performance.
We will compare these models on regression error metrics. Given the relatively moderate size of data (~7k samples), all these models are feasible in Colab.
	•	Features: We will use relevant features to predict total charges. We must be careful not to include churn as a feature in this regression, because if a customer churned, their total charges might be lower simply due to shorter tenure – including churn flag would trivially leak that information (since churned customers by definition have tenure <= current period). Instead, we use features known at customer acquisition or early in their life: demographic and service subscription info, contract type, etc., plus tenure and monthly charges as of the snapshot. However, note that TotalCharges is almost deterministically related to MonthlyCharges * Tenure for current customers (except churners who left before end of quarter might have slightly different relation). To make the regression less trivial, we might exclude one of these. For instance, predict TotalCharges using all features except itself (obviously) and excluding tenure – but that loses an important factor. Alternatively, exclude MonthlyCharges and try to predict total from tenure and services. However, for a realistic scenario, one might predict future lifetime value, but we lack future data in a snapshot. For simplicity, we will treat it as a straightforward regression and include tenure and monthly charges among inputs, acknowledging the model will learn something close to TotalCharges ≈ MonthlyCharges * Tenure. This still demonstrates the regression process.
	•	Training and tuning: Similar to classification, split the data (train/test) for regression (we can use the same split or a separate one if we want). Train the linear model and tree-based models on the training set. For linear regression, check assumptions (we might view residuals to see if non-linearity or heteroskedasticity is an issue). For tree/forest, tune hyperparameters (max depth, number of estimators, etc.) via cross-validation optimizing for RMSE or MAE. Feature selection here might involve removing highly correlated features to avoid multicollinearity (though tree-based models are robust to that). For interpretability, we can also examine feature coefficients in linear regression (e.g., it should show a roughly proportional relationship of ~monthly_charge for each additional unit of monthly charge and ~some amount per month of tenure).
	•	Outcome: A regression model that can predict total charges with reasonable accuracy. We will analyze which features most strongly influence the predictions. For instance, we expect contract type and add-on services to indirectly influence total charges (via monthly charges) – e.g., a two-year contract might come with consistent payments leading to high total charges if the customer stays throughout. We will evaluate the model on the test set using error metrics described later, and possibly visualize actual vs predicted charges to see how closely the model fits the data.
3. Clustering Analysis (Customer Segmentation)
	•	Method: We will apply K-Means clustering to the preprocessed data (without the churn label or other target). K-Means is chosen for its simplicity and effectiveness in many segmentation tasks[18]. The goal is to find natural groupings of customers. We might also consider hierarchical clustering or DBSCAN for comparison, but K-Means will be the primary method. If time permits, we could try a K-Prototypes algorithm (which allows clustering on mixed numeric and categorical data) as an extension, but the simpler route is to use K-Means on a fully numeric transformed dataset.
	•	Feature set for clustering: We will use a selection of features that make sense for clustering. To avoid high dimensionality and issues with categorical variables:
	•	One approach is to include all features after one-hot encoding and scaling. This gives the algorithm all information, but high dimensions (especially many dummy variables) can dilute distances.
	•	Another approach is to focus on key continuous features: e.g., tenure, monthly charges, number of services used (engineered feature), etc., and perhaps a few binary flags (like whether they have internet service or not). The Medium analysis of a similar telecom dataset, for example, used only continuous variables like tenure, monthly charge, bandwidth usage, etc., after removing categoricals[19]. We can attempt clustering with a comprehensive feature set but remain ready to adjust if results are not meaningful (e.g., if clusters end up separated by a single binary flag like “has internet or not” in a trivial way).
	•	In practice, we’ll likely try including major features (tenure, charges, number of services, contract length encoded numerically, etc.) and exclude customerID and any ID-like or target info.
	•	Choosing number of clusters (K): We will determine the optimal number of clusters using the Elbow Method and Silhouette analysis:
	•	Elbow Method: Run K-Means for a range of K values (e.g., 2 through 10) and compute the inertia or within-cluster sum of squares for each K. Plotting inertia vs K, we look for an “elbow point” where adding another cluster yields diminishing returns in reducing inertia[20]. That elbow suggests a suitable K (balance between simplicity and fidelity).
	•	Silhouette Score: For each K, also compute the average silhouette coefficient, which measures how well-separated the clusters are (a higher silhouette score means clusters are well apart and cohesive)[20][21]. We may plot silhouette scores vs K or even silhouette diagrams to validate cluster quality. A combination of elbow and silhouette helps choose K (e.g., we might see an elbow at K=3 or 4 and a high silhouette for those K).
	•	We expect perhaps a few meaningful segments – for example, maybe 3 segments like “Long-tenure high-value customers”, “Short-tenure low-spend customers”, and “Moderate tenure moderate spend with specific services”, but the data will tell.
	•	Cluster model fitting: Once K is chosen (say K = 3 for example), run K-Means again to obtain the final clusters. Because K-Means uses random initialization, run it a few times (scikit-learn’s default n_init=10 or higher) to ensure a stable result. All features were standardized earlier, which satisfies K-Means’ assumption of equal variance and avoids biasing by scale[22].
	•	Cluster interpretation: After clustering, we will profile each cluster:
	•	Calculate cluster centroids in terms of original feature scales (by inverse transforming the scaled values if needed) to interpret them. For example, one cluster’s centroid might show average tenure ~2 months, monthly charges ~$80, many with month-to-month contracts – indicating a cluster of new, high-paying customers who might be at risk of early churn. Another cluster might have centroid of tenure ~30 months, multiple services, lower churn rate, etc., indicating loyal customers with bundled services.
	•	For better interpretation, we’ll look at the distribution of original categorical features within each cluster (e.g., percentage of cluster on month-to-month vs two-year contracts, percentage using fiber optic internet, etc.).
	•	We will also check the churn label distribution per cluster (though we did not use churn to cluster, we can see after-the-fact how churn is represented in each group). This can yield valuable insights: for instance, if one cluster has disproportionately many churned customers, it means the clustering found a segment synonymous with high churn risk (e.g., “Cluster 1: mostly month-to-month, short-tenure customers, high churn rate” as noted in a similar analysis[23]).
	•	Outcome: The clustering will produce distinct customer segments and we will describe each segment in business terms. For example, we might label clusters as “Bargain Shoppers” vs “Loyal Bundlers” etc., based on characteristics. These segments can drive business actions: the company could target the high-risk segment with retention offers, or upsell the low-service segment to add more services (since having more services was linked to lower churn[14]).
Throughout modeling, we will use visualizations and cross-validation to ensure models generalize well. After each model is built, we’ll evaluate it on the test set (for supervised tasks) and interpret the results as described next.

Evaluation Metrics
Each task will use appropriate evaluation metrics to measure model performance, aligned with the objective of the task:
	•	Classification Metrics: For churn prediction (binary classification), we will evaluate models using:
	•	Accuracy: The overall proportion of correct predictions. However, with imbalanced classes, accuracy can be misleading (e.g., predicting all customers as “not churn” would be ~73.5% accurate given the churn rate[5], but that model is useless for catching churners).
	•	Precision: Among the customers predicted as churn, the percentage that actually churned. This measures how precise our model is in identifying true churners (important if we want to avoid false alarms in targeting customers).
	•	Recall (Sensitivity): Among the customers who truly churned, the percentage our model correctly identified. Recall is crucial for churn because missing a churner (false negative) means losing a customer without intervention. In this context, a high recall is often prioritized[17], since the cost of failing to retain a churner can be high. We will pay special attention to recall of the “Yes churn” class.
	•	F1-Score: The harmonic mean of precision and recall, providing a single metric that balances both. This is useful to compare models in an imbalanced scenario. We may particularly track the F1 for the churn class.
	•	ROC-AUC: The Area Under the ROC Curve gives a threshold-independent measure of separability. It’s useful for comparing models’ intrinsic ability to rank churn vs non-churn. A higher AUC means the model is better at assigning higher churn probabilities to actual churners than non-churners.
Our focus will be on recall and F1 for the positive class (churn). For example, we might prefer a model with slightly lower overall accuracy if it significantly boosts recall of churners[17]. In the evaluation, we’ll present the confusion matrix and classification report (precision, recall, F1) for the test set. If we experimented with an F-beta score (which weights recall more), we might report that as well (e.g., F2-score to emphasize recall as in some churn studies[24]). Ultimately, success in classification will be judged by how well we identify likely churners while keeping false alarms at a manageable level.
	•	Regression Metrics: For the regression task of predicting total charges, we will use standard regression error metrics:
	•	Mean Absolute Error (MAE): The average absolute difference between predicted and actual charges[25]. This is in the same units (dollars) and is easily interpretable (e.g., an MAE of 20 means on average our prediction is \$20 off from the true value).
	•	Mean Squared Error (MSE) and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): MSE is the average of squared errors, and RMSE is its square root, which brings it back to the original unit[25][26]. RMSE penalizes large errors more strongly due to the squaring, so it gives a sense of whether there are big outliers in error. We often focus on RMSE for interpretability (e.g., RMSE = 50 means an average error of \$50, giving more weight to big mistakes).
	•	R-squared (R²): The proportion of variance in the total charges explained by our model[27]. R² ranges from 0 to 1 (with 1 meaning perfect fit). It tells us how well the features collectively predict the target. We have to be cautious interpreting R² in our scenario, because a large part of total charges variance is trivially explained by tenure and monthly charges (so we expect a fairly high R²). We might also report Adjusted R² if we add many features, to account for the number of predictors (though with our dataset size and features count, it may not differ much from R²).
Our evaluation will include MAE and RMSE on the test set as the primary indicators of predictive error, and R² to indicate overall fit quality[28]. For example, we might find an MAE of \$20 and R² of 0.85 on the test set, meaning the model’s predictions are on average \$20 off and it explains 85% of the variation in customer total charges. We will aim for low error and high R², but we will also check for any bias (e.g., consistently overestimating charges for low-tenure customers) by analyzing residuals.
	•	Clustering Validation Metrics: Clustering is unsupervised, so evaluation is about the cohesion and separation of the clusters, as well as their business meaningfulness:
	•	Silhouette Coefficient: As mentioned, we will calculate the silhouette score for the clustering result. This metric ranges from -1 to 1; a higher value means the clusters are well separated and internally cohesive[21]. We will use average silhouette score to quantitatively assess the clustering quality for the chosen K, and possibly compare it with alternative K values. For instance, an average silhouette of 0.5 or above is decent; if we see silhouette dropping for higher K, it means adding clusters is creating less natural partitions.
	•	Inertia/WCSS: While inertia (within-cluster sum of squared distances) is used to find the elbow for K, it can also serve as a measure (lower inertia means tighter clusters). However, inertia always decreases with more clusters, so it’s not useful alone to evaluate one clustering result except in comparison to other K.
	•	Cluster Interpretability: A qualitative but crucial “metric.” We will evaluate how interpretable and actionable the clusters are. After forming clusters, we’ll check if each cluster can be described in a simple profile (e.g., “Cluster 2: Very low tenure, primarily month-to-month, high churn rate”). If clusters are too mixed or don’t make sense to domain experts, then even a high silhouette clustering might not be useful. We will document the characteristics of each segment and ensure they provide insight (this is part of evaluation in a business sense).
	•	Stability: If time permits, we might also test clustering stability by using different random seeds or a different clustering method (like hierarchical) to see if similar groups emerge. Consistent clusters across methods would validate that the segments are real patterns in data, not just an artifact of one algorithm’s randomness.
In summary, each task’s success will be measured with metrics suited to it: Precision/Recall/F1 for classification (with emphasis on churn recall)[17], error measures (MAE, RMSE) and R² for regression[25][27], and silhouette score plus interpretability for clustering[20]. We will present these metrics in the results section, often in table form for easy comparison (e.g., a table of classification metrics for each model, a table of regression errors, etc.).
Additionally, we will translate what these metrics mean in business terms for the audience. For example, “Our best churn model achieves 80% recall – it catches 80% of the customers who will churn, allowing marketing to reach out to most of them in advance (but with some false alarms).” Or “The regression model has an RMSE of \$50, which means on average our revenue predictions are within \$50 of the actual – acceptable accuracy for estimating customer value.” This way, metrics are tied to practical impact.
